TeamWise: the AI meeting facilitator

ETH Zurich

From scattered ideas to shared success—meetings made easy.

Challenge Overview

Problem Definition

Collaborative learning is known to improve student outcomes, such as higher achievement and engagement, when students work together toward a common goal [1] [2] [3]. However, in the ETH–ECH multi-campus context, supporting effective collaborative group work (CGW) is challenging. “Hybrid” classes, where some students are on one campus and others are on another, impose high cognitive and technical demands during group work [4].

Existing Solution Space & Gap

Under these conditions, well-structured teamwork is essential but difficult to achieve. Existing tools only partially address this need. Standard platforms, such as video conferencing, chat, and shared whiteboards, provide basic communication but lack explicit pedagogical support. For example, most Learning Management Systems (LMS) and collaboration systems offer only rudimentary features for group formation or monitoring [5]. Similarly, general tools like Zoom, Google Docs, and Miro allow for the sharing of ideas but provide no guidance on the group process.

Prior research in Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) and Computer-Supported Collaborative Learning (CSCL) has explored the use of smart helpers, such as chatbots and learning analytics, to aid group work; however, these often rely on static scripts or off-the-shelf dialogues that do not adapt to context [6] [7] [8]. Studies also show that students may not know how to collaborate effectively without guidance [9] [10].

In summary, there is a gap in the solution space: we lack tools that both structure collaboration (e.g., through design-thinking methods or collaboration scripts) and intelligently facilitate group processes in real time. TeamWise aims to fill this gap by combining a structured design process with AI assistance for remote student teams.

Design

TeamWise is an AI-facilitator platform enabling structured online collaboration for hybrid project teams. Designed for ETH/ECH university students in project-based courses, it generalizes to broader educational and professional contexts. The system uses a large language model (via Tavus.io) as a conversational facilitator and provides scaffolding for group meetings by creating agendas, guiding ideation steps, enforcing turn-taking, and summarizing discussions.

Key Capabilities

The AI facilitator offers several core functionalities to support the team’s workflow:
  • Structured Agendas & Workflow: TeamWise enables teams to collaboratively plan meeting agendas and follow project frameworks (e.g., design thinking stages) with clear goals and timing. This structured approach ensures each meeting has an explicit focus and helps prevent unproductive drifting [11] [12].
  • LLM-based Conversational Facilitation: An AI agent participates in the meeting via video, asking questions, prompting discussion, and enforcing equitable turn-taking. This virtual facilitator keeps conversations on track and ensures that all members are invited to contribute.
  • Ideation and Collaboration Tools: The platform supports creative tasks (e.g. brainstorming with the Double Diamond framework) by generating prompts and encouraging different viewpoints. It effectively serves as a digital “workshop assistant” that fosters both divergent and convergent thinking in group ideation.
  • Real-time Summarization: TeamWise captures key discussion points and automatically shares a summary at the end of the meeting. These recaps help participants recall decisions and track project progress, thus maintaining a shared understanding of the project status.
  • Engagement Balancing: To address unequal participation (a known challenge in online collaboration), TeamWise nudges quieter participants to share their ideas. By promoting balanced input, the system helps improve group satisfaction and collective learning [13].

In summary, TeamWise operationalizes CSCL (Computer-Supported Collaborative Learning) and HCI insights into a practical system that enhances remote teamwork.

Live Demo

The key feature demonstrated in the TeamWise prototype was an AI-facilitated live brainstorming session structured by the Double Diamond design framework. In this session, a conversational AI agent guided a small student team through a rapid, two-phase ideation process. The Double Diamond, a widely accepted design model, prescribes sequential divergent and convergent thinking phases – Discover and Define (followed by Develop and Deliver) – to explore problems and refine solutions. By opening with an explanation of the Double Diamond’s stages, the AI established a common process for collaboration. Such scaffolding aligns with TeamWise’s goal of promoting structured collaboration: the framework ensured the group first generated a wide range of ideas (Discover/divergent thinking) and then focused on selecting the most impactful idea (Define/convergent thinking).

Before the facilitation began, users were assigned a focused brainstorming challenge:

“In the next five years, what is the most impactful way AI can enhance learning?”

To set up the session, the user completed a brief meeting configuration form on the TeamWise platform, specifying:
  • Meeting Title: A descriptive name for the session
  • Duration: 5 minutes, to ensure we are within demo time limits
  • Participants: 2, as we had two laptops prepared for the demo
  • Meeting Type: “Brainstorming”
  • Method: “Double Diamond”
  • Meeting Goal: A clear statement of the assigned question
  • Comment: Participant roles and names for personalized facilitation
Optionally, teams could review the auto-generated agenda or attach supporting documents before submitting and joining the live session.

The system prompt and meeting plan directed the AI to perform specific facilitation tasks at each stage. In summary, the AI facilitator performed the following functions during the session:
  • Introduce Framework and Goals: At the outset, the AI verbally explained the Double Diamond process and the session’s goal, aligning participants on the task and methodology.
  • Prompt Idea Generation (Discover): The agent invited each team member to propose ideas about AI in learning, leveraging time prompts to keep the session moving. It encouraged rapid, creative contributions, consistent with divergent thinking.
  • Cluster and Prioritize (Define): After gathering ideas, the AI grouped similar ideas and guided the group toward identifying a single most impactful idea. This phase shifted from exploration to analysis, helping the team converge on a clear focus as recommended by the Double Diamond model.
  • Balance Participation: Throughout both phases, the AI monitored who had spoken and actively invited quieter voices to contribute, ensuring no one dominated the discussion. It reminded participants of the time remaining and refocused the conversation on the task.
  • Summarize and Close: Finally, the AI delivered a concise spoken summary of the discussion and next steps, reinforcing shared understanding. This verbal reflection helped to make learning explicit and ensured the group left with clear outcomes.
By automating these functions, the AI met the role of a facilitator in a hybrid meeting: managing the agenda, moderating discussion, tracking time, and ensuring inclusivity.

Technical Details

The website was built with React and Flask based on a Figma design. The bot was developed using the Tavus API, which utilizes Deepgram for speech recognition, OpenAI for language model understanding, and Cartesia for speech-to-text functionalities, offering a choice of voice, speed, and tone. The bot can be personalized based on the user’s choice of meeting method, as well as the meeting goal, agenda, and comments. It has the capability to take notes, summarize the meeting, and create a to-do list based on the meeting transcript. This API allows us to define a persona by using the user's information about the meeting as a prompt. Additionally, it provides near real-time transcription along with video and audio of the created persona.

Looking ahead, we envision a future where AI-driven meeting facilitators become commonplace in hybrid and remote work environments. As AI technology continues to advance, we anticipate that these facilitators will become increasingly sophisticated, capable of understanding complex discussions, recognizing emotional cues, and adapting their facilitation style to suit the needs of diverse teams. We foresee a future where these AI facilitators can seamlessly integrate with various collaboration tools, providing a unified experience that enhances productivity and engagement.

We believe that most conferencing tools will have integrated agentic bots that can make decisions on which tools to use—such as creating a Google Doc or a Figma page—while taking notes, creating summaries, and generating to-dos in real time based on the meeting transcript.

Design Process

Initial Directions & Inspiration

The TeamWise design process began by framing the well-documented challenges of hybrid, multi-campus group work. Prior research describes communication breakdowns, misaligned goals, and uneven participation in online teams [14] [15].

To resolve these pain points, the team focused on AI facilitation during online meetings. The initial design vision was for an “intelligent and conversational facilitator” that would recognize discussion topics in real time, prompt relevant questions, and guide the group using methods like the Double Diamond design framework. In short, the team’s early direction used insights from literature-based evidence of group-work issues and user input, setting a user-centered mandate for structured, active facilitation.

The concept evolved through iterative prototyping. The team sketched multiple facilitator designs (e.g., passive vs. active agents) and created low-fidelity “paper” prototypes in Balsamiq. These mockups were tested in a peer review session to elicit feedback. The passive prototype implemented a bot that provides an agenda, silently logs notes, and cues in the meeting interface. While peers appreciated the clear agenda and organization, they preferred more visible, real-time guidance. In response, the team selected to pursue the active facilitator: a bot who would speak prompts and guide discussions like a real-life facilitator.

Evolution Through Prototyping & Feedback

Technically, the team first attempted to build the conversational agent in-house, but the complexity of the project made it difficult to implement within the project timeline. To meet the MVP scope, the team pragmatically adopted Tavus.io’s LLM-powered video agent. Tavus provided a platform that could render a speaking avatar and handle natural language prompts. This external solution allowed the team to focus on content and flow rather than building all AI components from scratch. The resulting MVP supported a Double Diamond–guided brainstorming as the first meeting mode. In practice, TeamWise would introduce the meeting goal, run participants through the “Discover” and “Define” phases of Double Diamond, then “Develop” solution ideas – exactly as outlined in our design method.

User feedback underscored the importance of social presence in the virtual facilitator. It was confirmed during the feedback session that a silent helper was less engaging than a speaking agent. Based on this, TeamWise’s final design made the facilitator “present” and visible (e.g., an animated avatar) and prioritized spoken prompts. At the same time, careful balance was needed: overly frequent or verbose interventions became distracting, so the team tuned the script timing and provided mute/pause controls. Another key lesson was the value of customization. In trials, teams wanted to adjust the facilitator’s role before meetings (e.g., specifying goals, time limits, or turning certain phases on/off). As a result, the final design includes pre-meeting settings for agenda details and pacing. These refinements reflect the iterative, user-centered process: each prototype and test cycle revealed new needs that shaped the design.

The decision to switch from an internal agent build to Tavus’s solution was driven by practical constraints (time and feasibility) and aligned with the MVP scope. In this way, the team balanced innovation with pragmatism – delivering a working AI meeting facilitator that addresses the original goals of clarity, engagement, and structure in hybrid group work.

Key Pivots

  • Early theme alignment (Hybrid Learning): From the start, all team members were interested in the course’s Theme 1: Facilitating Hybrid Learning. This focus guided our goal to connect in-person and remote students inclusively. By aligning on this theme early, we framed all subsequent ideas in terms of supporting an equitable hybrid learning environment.
  • Clarifying pedagogical focus (Engagement vs. Collaboration): The team discussed whether our solution should target student–teacher interaction or student–student interaction, and whether to emphasize engagement (motivation, attention) versus collaboration (peer learning). Resolving this distinction helped us ideate with more granularity. For example, we noted that project-based learning (which emphasizes peer collaboration) tends to “foster deep thinking” through authentic group projects [16]. In other words, prioritizing student-to-student collaboration (rather than just teacher facilitation) better matched our learning objectives for hybrid project-based learning (PBL) settings.
  • Concept exploration and pivot to AI facilitation: Initially, we explored two routes: a holographic videoconferencing system and an AI “co-pilot” facilitator for lectures. After expert feedback (e.g., discussion with Dr. Nai Li on her successful AI teaching-assistant model), we realized the holographic idea was technically complex, whereas AI facilitation had strong pedagogical promise. This pivot was supported by emerging research: for instance, platforms like Khan Academy are integrating large language models to enable interactive Q&A and exploration, shifting education toward “more engaging, personalized, and adaptive” experiences [17]. Focusing on an AI facilitator aligned with this trend and seemed more feasible in the project timeline.
  • Refocusing on project-based collaboration: Rather than broadly supporting all aspects of hybrid learning or general project-based learning, the team strategically narrowed its scope to a highly specific and underserved challenge: AI-facilitated online meetings for hybrid teams. This focus emerged from both the literature review and team interest, where students expressed a desire for more structured and equitable experiences during remote collaboration. Instead of attempting to support full-course project management or asynchronous tools, TeamWise concentrates on real-time meeting facilitation—a critical but often neglected part of collaborative work in hybrid education.
This decision aligned both with user needs (who prioritized smoother meetings and better group dynamics) and with feasibility constraints, allowing the team to design and test a meaningful MVP. While project-based learning (PBL) remains a relevant context for TeamWise, the emphasis is not on managing the full PBL workflow, but rather on enabling more productive, inclusive synchronous interactions during group work.

By validating ideas against research, we ensured our facilitator design stayed grounded in evidence and real user needs.

Lessons Learned

  • Value of team diversity: Having a team with different backgrounds and skills was helpful. Each member’s unique perspective made our brainstorming sessions better, leading to more creative ideas. In our work, our different skills (like in AI, human-computer interaction, and teaching) helped us question our assumptions and improve our ideas from various viewpoints.
  • Embracing agile pivots: Being willing to change our plans early and often, without sticking too much to our first ideas, was very important. Each time we changed direction (like shifting from holograms to AI or from lectures to project-based learning), we discovered new opportunities and focused on what really mattered. Looking back, these adjustments saved us time and made it more likely that we would create something truly valuable.
  • Prioritizing needs-driven design: A key lesson we learned was to focus on creating something useful and based on research, rather than just going with our original idea. From the beginning, we looked into the real issues with hybrid learning and found evidence for what works best in teaching. This approach focused on understanding the needs of users and regularly checking our assumptions. For example, instead of assuming that a holographic solution was the answer, we assessed it based on its feasibility and learning benefits. We only moved to using AI when we had solid evidence and expertise to support that choice.
Our adjustments, based on expert advice, peer feedback, and relevant studies, helped us better meet real learning needs. By focusing on the hybrid learning theme early on, defining our teaching approach, and using feedback and research to guide our decisions, TeamWise followed a user-centered approach to educational technology.
